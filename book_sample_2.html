The Heartless Romantic

 

 

By Connor Prime

 

Part 1: The Heartless Romantic

 

1

 

Thursday, November 6

Senior Year

 

What do you think of when you think of heartless men? Do you think of the cold, steady confessions of Jeffery Dahmer or the mad, frenzied, manipulative movements of Adolf Hitler? Or perhaps you may think of the chilling indifference of Joseph Stalin as he refused to negotiate for the life of his captured son? I am none of these things, and yet, I know I am heartless. It is part of my very being, a fucked-up piece of my wiring, which is, by no means, my fault, yet a setback I had to live with.

I do not feel the weight of others pain. Once, an acquaintance of mine fell over in a game of soccer, and he twisted his ankle. I held him up, but as he screamed in pain, my nerves remained calm. This is not (as far as I know) the result of any immediate trauma. True, my mom died, but I did not cry then. I was not in the crash. I heard of it and continued playing with my toys, understanding there was nothing I could do to save her. I was too young to understand the insult to my living father.

My father, a distant lawyer, was probably more distant due to his horror at my indifference. But my tears would not have saved her, and knowing I performed for him would have made my father hate me more.

I digress.

Heartlessness is emotionally out of sync, or at least that is what I believe it to mean. It is the absence of something. Not the presence of something that many associate with it. I could say it means lacking empathy, but what good are these words with the definitions we have?

Empathy, as far as I care, is the physiological mimicking of other peoples brainwaves. Its just the mimicking of brainwaves of those of the same species, as it was first discovered in monkeys. Empathy is not kindness. It is not required for kindness. It is a guideline that helps one be kind. I might have some, but admittedly I have little, if any at all. My lawyerish behavior is by no means a mere reflection of my father. Definitions are important.

So, what do you do when you dont have those guardrails for kindness? I made my own. I thought empathy was like love, and lacking it, I took the next best thing: honor. I created a code around minimizing harm and maximizing mutual benefit. And it was fine. For a long time, it worked. I had no need for that silly thing called love. And then I fell in love with another.

There was a girl who I respected. She was relatively quiet but spoke deliberately, directly, and insightfully in class. She cared about the subject, and that made me care about her. Some people go through the motions. They pass through without engaging. Im normally not one of them. And neither was Samantha. Sam was different.

I had few friends at the time, and my father was insisting I be more social. I imagined friendship as something gradual. It was a game where the goal was to make meaningful connections. If you lost, it was a game, and once you got a friend, more would come as a chain reaction. I tracked my victories and defeats, asking myself if this person and that person were compatible and, if so, how to connect without being too passive or bold. I rarely cared to pursue a friendship, let alone a romantic relationship for that matter, for I was happy, but Sam was an exception. She gave off an aura I wanted to be around.

I spent two or three weeks plotting our first interaction. I had no projects, and I dont do well without projects, so our possible relationship became a project. People make tough projects, for it seems often a failure is unrepairable. People, as far as I knew, got along due to similarities, so I made a mental note of Sams qualities, though it was a rough sketch at best.

I didnt know the essence of who she was and hadnt a clue, but like a solid first chapter, what I did know was luring me in. Of the niche and overlapping interests we both had (language, history, and intellectual discussion), I figured the overlap would be embarrassing if we didnt know each other well. So, I took a risk.

I asked my friend Gabe to get ice cream, and then I asked Sam to join us. Then, after class, I asked Gabe if I could ask her to get ice cream with us. All parties said yes, but Gabe was annoyed at my blatant ploy.

So, Ive been drafted as a wingman, he said. Or rather, Ive been sucked in as a buffer zone, like the Iron Curtain.

I think we can all use a change of pace, I said.

How altruistic, Gabe said.

I paused, thinking now was not the time for comebacks, but the thing was, I didnt feel sorry.

Listen, I said, I didnt mean to do this.

Then what did you mean to do? Gabe asked, used to my manipulative behavior.

How about this? Well get tacos this weekend. Just us.

Are you buying? Gabe asked.

I considered this for half a second. No.

What an asshole, he said, but I knew he was joking. It all worked as planned. Gabe agreed to be a buffer zone because he had a hard time saying no. The offer appeared casual, and I made amends without bribing Gabe with tacos, only with camaraderie. Now for the talking.

People generally make me nervous, and new people I look up to especially so. I felt I had to prove myself worth Sams time. School ended after my talk with Gabe, and we met by the stairs to the entrance. A cold shadow filled my heart as I saw Sam. I felt unworthy.

I gestured toward Sam with my head as she approached.

Thats Sam, I said.

Gabe looked around for a second and yelled, Sam!

I instantly face-palmed.

Y-yes, Sam replied, nervous at his excitement.

There she is, Gabe said.

Hey, I said, noticing her pinkish-orange blouse and short black hair. A moment of silence passed. RIP my social life. I gestured toward Gabe.

Ill like to introduce you to my friend, Gabe.

Gabe stuck out his hand.

Gabriel Ramirez. Pleasure to meet you, he said.

I smiled nervously. In fact, I did almost every action nervously at this point. From there, Gabe carried the conversation for the next few minutes while I recovered my wits. I was shrinking inside. Nonetheless, the sound of Gabes voice held me in. A car drove by in front of me as we stood waiting for a red light. I blinked.

So, we have two options, I said as I smiled. First, theres the nice but expensive hipster ice cream. Then theres Dryers. Im fine either way.

What about Safeway ice cream? Gabe asked.

Where would we get spoons? I asked. And where would we eat?

The answer was at my house, but that had other complications.

Oh, Gabe said, then Dryers.

Dryers, Sam said.

Dryers it is, I said.

We headed toward Dryers in a now comfortable silence before Sam spoke, which was good because I was hopelessly cycling through things to say.

So, Gabe just told me youre good at chess, she said.

Yes, its the only sport Im good at, I said.

Sam smiled, saying, Its no longer an Olympic sport.

So, is checkers now? Gabe joked. Cause Im a lot better at checkers.

Sam and I smiled. My smile was slighter, but I felt a warm glow. Its stupid, sometimes silly, to hold onto these things too much, but Ill take any victory I can get. I felt like I had found a weak spot in the armor that protects people from a decent conversation.

Are you any good? I asked.

Is that a challenge? Sam asked.

Only if you want it to be.

Ron was one of the best in elementary school, Gabe said.

Sam looked at me mischievously. Well, this isnt elementary school.

Well, beating you will be . . . I paused for dramatic effect.

Dont say it, Sam said.

Okay, you know I wont . . . elementary! I said. I even added finger-snap guns.

Youre terrible, Sam said.

But not at chess, I said, with extra finger guns.

Put on your cool-guy glasses, Gabe said.

Damn straight, I said, running into a pole as I turned toward them.

I fell to the ground.

Are you okay? Sam asked once she and Gabe lifted me up.

Im fine. I just lose my mind sometimes, I said without thinking.

Sam paused and looked at me with pity as a few things went through my head. The first was that Id just stabbed myself in the back, which felt great. The second was that the phrase lose your mind is stupid because, to me, it means to stop thinking, but it can also mean to go insane for most people, which can involve a lot of fucking thinking. The third was, Whats up with this cool person pitying me? Thats not cool.

Its okay, Sam said. There was a long, awkward pause. She neither looked guilty nor casual about it, which I thought meant she didnt understand.

You ran into a pole, Gabe said as I scrambled through damage control, but Gabe was already on it. Thats not losing your mind. Its just being stupid, and Im afraid its incurable.

I smiled at the joke, which was surprisingly reassuring.

Gabe, Sam said disappointedly.

Now ladies, I said, you can both cool it. I dont need anyone defending my honor.

Sam looked at me while Gabe maintained his consistent smile.

Now, lets get some ice cream, I said.

I started walking. Gabe followed, and after a split second of hesitation, so did Sam.

Its fine if you want to talk about it, Sam said.

Its a simple dilemma, I said. And its mine.

What a toxic man, Gabe said sarcastically.

I smiled. Gabe thrived on momentum, and so did I. I looked back.

But if you want to help, tell me if theres a pole next time.

Looking back wont help, Gabe said as I turned back around.

I smiled. The day passed with Gabe and me doing most of the talking while Sam remained relatively quiet, licking her ice cream and then staring dully at it. There was something she wanted to say but couldnt. I felt a little bad, like Id dismissed her, and tried to welcome her back into the conversation, but I only got a few short sentences. Eventually, we left the ice cream place. Gabe was about to head home, and Sam was headed to the bus stop. I offered to walk with her. Gabe waved us off as the two of us walked.

So how do you do it? Sam asked.

Do what? I said, caught off guard.

You know, trudge forward. Stumble and recover so easily.

I lifted an eyebrow. Mild exaggeration.

Sam smiled slightly. You know what I mean. You turned that conversation around like it was nothing.

Practice, I said. Practice and necessity.

It wouldnt put me off if you just said what you felt.

I didnt know. And I didnt want to risk it.

We were about three minutes from the bus stop.

So, what did you want out of this?

This? I asked. I wanted a good conversation, though thats kind of counterintuitive.

We were a little more than half a block from the bus stop.

Dont worry, Sam said. Im used to it.

Used to what?

People thinking I care too much.

No such thing, I said. People just need to care more rationally.

And what does that mean?

I paused as the bus came up.

Well have to finish this another time, she said.

I stood quietly as the doors opened, and she walked toward them.

Okay, I said as Sam walked inside and the doors closed. I saw her wave through the window as I smiled and waved back. Then I shook my head when she was out of view. I would never understand that woman. And the problem was, I wanted to.

 

 

 

So far, the night had passed like any other night. Homework, watching YouTube, a quiet dinner with my father, and then my brain playing the replay of my life, but this time with more commentary. I began to analyze the dynamics of the trio of Sam, Gabe, and I.

Sam wanted to have some kind of deep conversation. Gabe wanted to keep me away from topics I didnt express interest in pursuing. Gabe was always a good cheerleader. If I needed raw energy and humor to keep me up, I could trust him. I saw the opposing ideologies in what I thought was clarity. Then I started to feel guilty.

I used Gabe as a buffer zone and then left him to walk home alone. My head created an image of me standing by Gabe while Sam went farther away. In this image, I knew chasing her would cost my friendship with Gabe. I shook my head.

This was a false extreme, I thought with a calmer mind but unsettled heart. They could be in the same room and be seen separately without favoritism.

 

 

 

After some time, I decided it was best to sort things out as uncomplicatedly as possible, which is something that is against my nature. All that happened today was this:

 

I spoke to a girl from class today. She was interesting. I worry this will hurt my friendship, but I hope it wont. Probably overthinking things.

 

Its time to sleep now, but that is much easier said than done.
